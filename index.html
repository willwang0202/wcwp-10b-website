⚡ Tension

The Cost of Playing Live

After losing money four consecutive times trying to host my own shows, I started asking a question I could not understand: why is it so hard for musicians to get on a stage without getting broke first?

The Problem

💸

Money Gone Before the Show

Artists have to pay deposits for gear, promotion fees, and staff. All this money is gone before the show, and we can't really predict the exact audience size until people attend the show.

📉

80 People, $350 Lost

We expect 100–120 people to fill the room that has a rent over $1,600, but it was only about 80 people attending, losing about $350 for us.

🚫

Genuinely Prohibitive

For a local or emerging artist without a large fan base, these conditions are not just inconvenient; they are genuinely prohibitive.

The Research

🍺

Belly Up Tavern

What stayed with me long after our conversation was the fundamental shift in who absorbs the financial risk of an event that didn't sell well. The venue's overhead did not change because Spotify, Apple Music, or Deezer exist.

✊

Che Cafe, UCSD

Maddie described the Che not just as a music venue but as a proof of the concept of community-driven space, not a profit-driven business.

The Conrad

The Conrad had actually doubled both audience and revenue, because the people finding their artists online were genuine fans who had discovered a specific performer, followed their work, and traveled specifically to see them live.
